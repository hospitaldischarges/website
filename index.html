                “Why are hospitals unable to discharge patients?”

                What did we do?

                We analysed a full year of NHS data showing the number of people who are classed as fit to leave hospital but are unable to because of delays at the close of each day.

                Data was available for each acute hospital trust in England, 164 in total, between April 2022 and April 2023 inclusive.

                How many people face delays in leaving hospital each day?

                On an average day, around 23,000 people are classed as medically fit for discharge in acute hospitals in England.

                To give that a sense of scale - that is around the population of Whitehaven in Cumbria or Hinckley in Leicestershire.

                However, at the end of the day around 13,200 remain in a bed - that equates to about 58% of people classed as fit to leave.

                That increases to around 70% on a Sunday.

                Which areas are seeing the longest delays?

                The proportion of people who do not leave a hospital bed despite being given the all-clear to do so differs greatly depending on the hospital.

                For example, on an average day at Liverpool University Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust, 313 patients, or 92% of those eligible for discharge, remain in a bed overnight. That is compared to just 24% of patients at Bradford Teaching Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust.

                A quarter of the trusts in our study had at least one day where no inpatient eligible for discharge in the entire hospital left their bed. Mid and South Essex NHS Foundation Trust had 42 such days.

                On a regional level, we can see that patients were about 20% more likely to face delays in the North West than in the East of England. The table shows the aggregated days where a patient was classed as eligible for discharge but did not do so by the end of that day across the English regions.

                Is it getting worse?

                With the data available we were able to compare the last four weeks of April in 2022 to the last four weeks of April in 2023, to see if the proportion of people facing delays increased. These are classed as weeks 14-17 of the calendar year. We could also compare weeks 15-18 (from 10 April to 8 May) in both 2022 and 2023.

                The results paint a mixed picture in England. Nationally, the volume of daily discharge delays increased by 1% comparing weeks 14-17 over the two years and by 2% comparing weeks 15-18. To give you a sense of scale - there were 351,702 aggregated delays of more than a day in April 2022 and 356,259 in April 2023, a rise of 4,557 extra delayed days.

                However, breaking this figure down by region we can see England was split - with areas such as London and the South East seeing big rises, though from a lower starting base - and areas such as the South West seeing big falls.
